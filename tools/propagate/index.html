How it works

Each measured quantity carries a standard uncertainty (±1σ). Assuming the inputs are independent, the uncertainty of f is the root-sum-square of each variable's contribution — the classic first-order propagation law:

σ_f = √( Σ (∂f/∂xᵢ · σᵢ)² )

The partial slopes are found numerically with a symmetric difference at ±σ (exact for linear and quadratic forms, accurate otherwise — no symbolic differentiation, no eval). The error budget ranks each term's share of the total variance, so you can see at a glance which measurement to improve first. Math constants pi, tau are built in; physical constants (c, h, hbar, kB, NA, qe, eps0, mu0, me, g) auto-fill as editable rows — so you can give a measured g its own uncertainty, or override c. Functions: sqrt, exp, ln, log10, sin, cos, tan, atan2, sinh…, pow, hypot and more.
